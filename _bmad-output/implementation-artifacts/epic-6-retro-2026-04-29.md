## 3. What Went Well

1. **The command lifecycle is now traceable as a system concept.** Story 6.1 verified API, MediatR, actor, persistence, publication, dead-letter, query-status, and replay activities across the two registered ActivitySources.

2. **The trace model handles the DAPR actor boundary.** The explicit traceparent/tracestate fallback through `CommandEnvelope.Extensions` is important because actor invocation can otherwise break visual trace continuity.

3. **Structured logging moved from convention to enforceable shape.** Story 6.2 verified source-generated LoggerMessage usage, EventId ranges, stage fields, correlation/causation IDs, log levels, and payload protection.

4. **Security-relevant operational signals improved.** Authorization failures, tenant mismatches, origin context, SourceIP, failure layer, and dead-letter correlation are now part of the observable system behavior.

5. **Health and readiness have a clear operational contract.** `/health`, `/alive`, and `/ready` have separate meanings, tag predicates, status-code mappings, and DAPR dependency coverage.

6. **The team corrected issues discovered during verification.** Story 6.3 did not stop at documentation; it added cancellation-token propagation tests, null-guard tests, config-store wrong-type coverage, and response-writer fault handling.

7. **The older five-story Epic 6 artifacts were preserved as evidence.** Even though sprint-status now tracks three canonical stories, historical records provide useful detail on dead-letter origin tracing and the original health/readiness split.

---

## 4. What Struggled

1. **Epic 6 inherited unresolved runtime-confidence debt.** Live command-surface verification, Tier 3 pub/sub delivery, and drain integrity coverage were already raised in earlier retros and remain open.

2. **Verification story records are split across two epic structures.** The current sprint-status model has three stories, while older artifacts describe five. This makes retrospective evidence stronger but status interpretation harder.

3. **Some observability claims remain unit-level or audit-level.** Story records cite manual Aspire inspection for some dashboard behaviors, but this retrospective did not rerun AppHost or capture fresh dashboard evidence.

4. **Structured logging had acceptable deviations from the architecture table.** Actor activation logs `commandType` instead of `currentSequence`, and domain rejection uses `Stage` rather than an explicit `rejectionEventType`. These are documented and defensible, but future readers need the rationale.

5. **Health/readiness classification has product tradeoffs.** Pub/sub and config-store failures return Degraded/200 by design. That preserves command acceptance but can hide downstream lag unless operators also watch backlog/drain signals.

6. **Pre-existing unrelated test failures keep appearing in story notes.** Several records mention unrelated failures, which is useful honesty but also creates noise unless each story clearly separates blocking defects from environmental or prior issues.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Observability is most useful when it follows the domain lifecycle.** Epic 6 tied telemetry to command submission, actor processing, persistence, publication, dead-lettering, status query, and replay.

2. **Verification stories still create implementation work.** Story 6.3 shows that audit-driven work can surface real hardening gaps even when the feature already exists.

3. **Correlation and causation are now product capabilities.** They are not just log fields; they enable replay diagnosis, dead-letter investigation, and tenant-safe operator workflows.

4. **Health and readiness need operator semantics, not just endpoints.** Degraded readiness is intentional, but it must be paired with backlog and publication visibility.

5. **Retro follow-through needs a stronger gate.** High-priority items from Epic 5 remained open through Epic 6, so future retros should explicitly reconcile prior commitments before closing.
